Robotic Challenge

The Robot Challenge is designed to give advanced robotics training to undergraduates with background in cognitive science and computer science. The teams operate independently at every phase, from design to final production. Each team has a budget for ordering parts and, combined with supplies and expertise provided by the Interdisciplinary Robotics Research Laboratory and the Science Technology Support Department, create their robots from scratch. Students develop their projects for the challenge (formerly known as the competition) over the course of the Spring semester and receive .5 credit for this independent study.

The 2014 Challenge

This year, faculty at Vassar were awarded a major grant from the National Science Foundation that will allow us to explore fundamental questions in what we are calling Evolutionary Developmental Robotics (EvoDevo Robotics for short). The faculty on this project include Ken Livingston, John Long, Marc Smith, Jodi Schwarz, Nick Livingston, and Josh Bongard of the University of Vermont. This year's challenge involves teams of students interested in taking on the challenge of solving one of the more interesting problems associated with this project. That problem is how to build effective codes for constructing an intelligent agent, or what we are calling G-to-P maps.

Think of the problem this way. The DNA in a biological system (the "G") is an instruction manual for building an organism (the "P"). Put that DNA in the right environment and what you have is a manufactory for life. In the larger project we are working on, we are attempting to understand why some kinds of G-to-P maps are more evolvable than others, a problem that has many facets. We are challenging each of this year's teams to come up with a G-to-P mapping system that can serve as the heart of an artificial manufactory for robotic agents (both real and simulated).

Because this problem takes its inspiration from biological systems but uses robotic systems to test hypotheses, our teams include people with the broadest possible range of skills and the ability to learn to work across traditional disciplinary lines. Participants have backgrounds in cognitive science, robotics or AI (e.g., Cognitive Science of Perception & Action, Computer Science), physics, and biology (especially genetics, neuroscience, and developmental biology). We look forward to seeing their results!
